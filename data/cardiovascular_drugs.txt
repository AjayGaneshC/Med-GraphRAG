# Cardiovascular Disease and Drug Interactions

## Hypertension Treatment

Hypertension, or high blood pressure, is defined as systolic blood pressure ≥130 mmHg or diastolic blood pressure ≥80 mmHg. It is a major risk factor for cardiovascular disease, stroke, and kidney disease.

### First-Line Antihypertensive Agents

**ACE Inhibitors** (Angiotensin-Converting Enzyme Inhibitors):
- Lisinopril, enalapril, ramipril
- Mechanism: Block conversion of angiotensin I to angiotensin II
- Side effects: Dry cough, angioedema, hyperkalemia
- Contraindicated in pregnancy

**ARBs** (Angiotensin II Receptor Blockers):
- Losartan, valsartan, irbesartan
- Mechanism: Block angiotensin II receptors
- Benefits: Similar efficacy to ACE inhibitors without cough
- Side effects: Hyperkalemia, hypotension

**Calcium Channel Blockers**:
- Amlodipine, nifedipine (dihydropyridines)
- Diltiazem, verapamil (non-dihydropyridines)
- Mechanism: Block L-type calcium channels in vascular smooth muscle
- Side effects: Peripheral edema (dihydropyridines), bradycardia (non-dihydropyridines)

**Thiazide Diuretics**:
- Hydrochlorothiazide, chlorthalidone
- Mechanism: Inhibit sodium reabsorption in distal convoluted tubule
- Side effects: Hypokalemia, hyperuricemia, hyperglycemia

## Heart Failure Management

Heart failure is a clinical syndrome caused by structural or functional cardiac abnormalities resulting in reduced cardiac output. Treatment depends on ejection fraction.

### Heart Failure with Reduced Ejection Fraction (HFrEF)

Guideline-directed medical therapy includes:

1. **ACE Inhibitors or ARBs**: Reduce mortality and hospitalization
   - Lisinopril, enalapril for ACE inhibitors
   - Valsartan, losartan for ARBs

2. **Beta-Blockers**: Reduce mortality in stable HFrEF
   - Carvedilol, metoprolol succinate, bisoprolol
   - Mechanism: Block beta-adrenergic receptors, reduce heart rate and myocardial oxygen demand
   - Must be started at low doses and titrated slowly

3. **ARNI** (Angiotensin Receptor-Neprilysin Inhibitor):
   - Sacubitril/valsartan (Entresto)
   - Superior to ACE inhibitors for reducing mortality
   - Should not be used with ACE inhibitors (36-hour washout required)

4. **Mineralocorticoid Receptor Antagonists**:
   - Spironolactone, eplerenone
   - Reduce mortality when added to ACE inhibitor and beta-blocker
   - Monitor for hyperkalemia

5. **SGLT2 Inhibitors**:
   - Dapagliflozin, empagliflozin
   - Reduce hospitalization and mortality regardless of diabetes status
   - Mechanism: Promote natriuresis and osmotic diuresis

6. **Diuretics**: For symptom relief and fluid management
   - Furosemide (loop diuretic) is most commonly used
   - Does not improve mortality

## Anticoagulation for Atrial Fibrillation

Atrial fibrillation increases stroke risk 5-fold. Anticoagulation decisions are based on CHA2DS2-VASc score.

### Direct Oral Anticoagulants (DOACs)

DOACs are preferred over warfarin for most patients:

**Factor Xa Inhibitors**:
- Rivaroxaban: Once daily dosing, take with food
- Apixaban: Twice daily, lowest bleeding risk
- Edoxaban: Once daily, requires prior heparin

**Direct Thrombin Inhibitors**:
- Dabigatran: Twice daily, requires renal dose adjustment
- Has reversal agent (idarucizumab)

### Warfarin

- Vitamin K antagonist
- Requires INR monitoring (target 2.0-3.0)
- Multiple drug and food interactions
- CYP2C9 and VKORC1 genetic variants affect dosing

## Critical Drug Interactions

### Cardiovascular Drug Interactions

1. **ACE inhibitors + Potassium supplements**: Risk of hyperkalemia
2. **ACE inhibitors + NSAIDs**: Reduced antihypertensive effect, acute kidney injury
3. **Beta-blockers + Verapamil/Diltiazem**: Severe bradycardia, heart block
4. **Digoxin + Amiodarone**: Increased digoxin levels (reduce digoxin dose by 50%)
5. **Statins + Fibrates**: Increased myopathy risk (especially gemfibrozil)
6. **Warfarin + Antibiotics**: Altered INR (especially fluoroquinolones, metronidazole)

### QT Prolongation Risk

Multiple drugs can prolong the QT interval and cause torsades de pointes:
- Amiodarone, sotalol, dofetilide (antiarrhythmics)
- Haloperidol, ziprasidone (antipsychotics)
- Fluoroquinolones, macrolides (antibiotics)
- Ondansetron (antiemetic)

Combined use of QT-prolonging drugs significantly increases arrhythmia risk.

## Lipid Management

Statins are the cornerstone of lipid-lowering therapy for cardiovascular risk reduction:

**High-Intensity Statins** (LDL reduction ≥50%):
- Atorvastatin 40-80 mg
- Rosuvastatin 20-40 mg

**Moderate-Intensity Statins** (LDL reduction 30-49%):
- Atorvastatin 10-20 mg
- Rosuvastatin 5-10 mg
- Simvastatin 20-40 mg

**Statin Intolerance**: Alternative therapies include:
- Ezetimibe: Inhibits intestinal cholesterol absorption
- PCSK9 inhibitors (evolocumab, alirocumab): Monoclonal antibodies, very effective but expensive
- Bempedoic acid: ATP citrate lyase inhibitor, can be used in statin-intolerant patients

### Statin Drug Interactions

Statins metabolized by CYP3A4 (atorvastatin, simvastatin, lovastatin) have significant interactions:
- Grapefruit juice: Increases statin levels
- Clarithromycin, erythromycin: Increase statin levels
- Cyclosporine: Dramatically increases statin levels
- Diltiazem, verapamil: Moderate increase in statin levels

Rosuvastatin and pravastatin have fewer drug interactions due to limited CYP metabolism.
